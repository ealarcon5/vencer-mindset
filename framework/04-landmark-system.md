# Phase 4: The Landmark System

## What This Phase Does

Landmarks are the permanent territory of your world. Unlike regular content (a video that has a good week and then fades), a landmark is a **core idea you have named, claimed, and built a system around** — it is evergreen, it grows in relevance the more you reference it, and it does two things that nothing else in your content strategy can do:

1. **Zoomed-out context** — a new visitor immediately understands they've entered a world, not just another content channel
2. **Resonance filtering** — it deeply attracts the right people and repels the wrong ones, because a landmark embeds your values and worldview, not just useful information

**Time:** 60–90 minutes (per landmark concept — you'll brainstorm several and develop 1–2 fully)

**Prerequisites:** Phases 1–3 complete.

---

## What Makes a Landmark

```
A landmark is NOT:
✗  A good video
✗  A useful framework
✗  An interesting take
✗  A popular post

A landmark IS:
✓  A named concept that belongs to your world specifically
✓  A claim about how you see things — not just how things are
✓  Something that, when encountered, immediately signals
   "this person has a whole world behind this idea"
✓  A piece of content that functions as a map of your world
   for people who just arrived
```

**Examples from other creators:**
- Dan Koe's "Human 3.0" — an entire philosophy about self-authorship under one core ID, with visuals, language, and a system behind it
- Paul Graham's "Maker Schedule vs. Manager Schedule" — a single idea that reframes how a specific type of person (makers) should think about their time
- The video that prompted this framework: "Digital DaVinci" — a named archetype with a visual history (Da Vinci), a claim (generalists win in the AI era), and a system (world building)

Notice that each of these contains language you start using after you encounter them. That is the mark of a true landmark — it gives you a new word or phrase for something you already felt but couldn't name.

---

## The Three Components of a Landmark

Every strong landmark has three layers:

```
1. LANGUAGE       The name, the terms, the vocabulary that comes with it.
                  People start using your words. This is how you know the
                  landmark is working.

2. VISUALS        The imagery, metaphors, or aesthetic that makes the
                  concept tangible and memorable. "Digital DaVinci" works
                  partly because Leonardo da Vinci is a powerful visual
                  anchor.

3. SYSTEM         The underlying framework, model, or process that gives
                  the concept structure. This is what makes it teachable
                  and expandable. A landmark without a system is just
                  a clever name.
```

## The Landmark Map

Once you have 2–3 landmarks developed, map how they connect. A strong world has landmarks that reference each other — each one deepens the same overarching vision from a different angle.

```
Landmark 1: _____________________
     │
     │ (leads to / unlocks)
     ▼
Landmark 2: _____________________
     │
     │ (leads to / unlocks)
     ▼
Landmark 3: _____________________
     │
     │ (leads to)
     ▼
[Your Offer]
```

This is how your content becomes a journey rather than a collection of videos. Someone who enters through Landmark 1 naturally wants Landmark 2. By Landmark 3, they understand your worldview completely — and they are either your ideal client or they've self-selected out.

---

## The Evergreen Property

Unlike regular content, landmarks appreciate. Every new piece of content you create that references a landmark increases that landmark's value retrospectively. In 20 videos, you can still reference Landmark 1 — and new viewers will go find it, and it will still be relevant, still building trust.

This is the content compounding effect: instead of building a content library where each piece competes for attention, you are building a world where each piece points to the architecture.
